research interests

Sea-surface height is a fundamental variable in physical oceanography and is one of few variables observable globally using satellite altimetry. Sea-surface height is a complicated manifestation of many processes both within and at the surface of the ocean, spanning a wide range of spatial and temporal scales, and as such is complicated and difficult to predict. This prediction, however, is necessary as anthropogenic emissions of fossil fuels leads to a changing climate and, invariably, a rising of the sea level. It is well known that tides are of first order importance in sea-surface height, but what about variability outside of this very narrow frequency band? To study the lower-frequency variability--the mesoscale phenomena and submesoscale phenomena--using satellite altimeters such as the Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) satellite, the tides must first be accurately removed, as high-frequency signals such as tides are known to alias to lower frequencies and contaminate the temporally subsampled altimetry data. At high-frequencies, including the tidal band, internal tides and internal waves are also of great interest. To achieve this level of understanding of the internal wave continuum, both highly realistic ocean models and in-situ observations are used. In-situ observations provide real ocean data, while ocean models allow for global coverage. Aliasing of the internal wave continuum will also contaminate the lower frequency variability and must again be removed from the satellite data before study of the lower frequency variability begins, but the internal wave continuum is intrinsically of great interest as it is known to play a large role in ocean mixing. This ocean mixing is believed to participate greatly in the moderating of global temperature and has dissipated much of the excess carbon dioxide added to the climate system through human activity over the last century.
